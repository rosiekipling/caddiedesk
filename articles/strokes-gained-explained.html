Strokes Gained, explained

918 words · June 2026 · Rosie Kipling

How a maths professor cracked golf's most important question: which part of a player's game is actually winning them tournaments?

You can tell a golfer is good — but what's actually making them good? Until 2011, this was surprisingly hard to answer. Driving distance told you how far someone hit it, not whether that helped them score. Greens-in-regulation rewarded landing on the green, regardless of whether you'd left yourself a 6-foot birdie putt or a 60-foot lag.

Mark Broadie, a maths professor at Columbia, changed that with his strokes-gained framework. He spent years analysing ShotLink data and developed the system in his 2014 book Every Shot Counts. The PGA Tour adopted it shortly after.

Strokes gained gives every shot a value: how many strokes it gained or lost against what a tour pro would average from the same situation. Apply it across every shot in a round, and you get a precise picture of where a player's edge lives. Here's how it works.

Introducing Strokes Gained

The idea is simple: for every shot, compare what the player did to what a tour pro would average from the same situation. The difference is the strokes gained or lost.

A worked example

Imagine a 165-yard approach shot from the fairway. Before the player swings, the question is: how many shots would a tour pro typically need to finish the hole from here?

The expected strokes from 165 yards in the fairway is 3.02. That's what an average tour pro would take to hole out from here.

The player hits a great 7-iron. The ball lands 12 feet from the pin. The expected strokes from 12 feet is 1.66.

Expected strokes after 1.66
Plus the shot itself +1
Strokes gained +0.36

The shot took 1 stroke and reduced the expected strokes to hole out by 1.36. So the player gained 0.36 strokes on this shot vs. what an average tour pro would have done.

Apply this across every shot in every round, and you get a precise picture of where a player's advantage lives. Did they gain those strokes off the tee? On approach? Around the green? On the putting surface? The system tells you exactly where each player's edge or weakness is.

But all of this depends on one critical input: the expected strokes value. Where do those numbers come from?

Expected Strokes vs. reality

Where do the "expected strokes" come from?

Every yardage, lie, and putt distance has an expected outcome — the number of strokes a tour pro typically needs to hole out from that situation. These baselines are calculated from years of ShotLink data: every shot, every distance, every lie, recorded since 2003.

So when the data says "expected strokes from 165 yards in the fairway is 3.02", that's not an estimate. It's an empirical fact derived from the tens of thousands of approaches tour pros have hit from that distance and lie.

Why the curve isn't a straight line

The curve is piecewise: steepest where it matters most. Under 100 yards, every yard closer translates to a better birdie chance. From 100-200 yards, tour pros hit greens consistently — about 70% of the time — and the resulting putts are similar. Beyond 200 yards, the green-in-regulation rate drops sharply (around 50% at 200 yards, 30% at 250). Players are still aiming at the green, but missing means an extra chip and putt added to the score. The steeper slope reflects the rising cost of those misses.

Putting

Putting follows its own curve. A 3-foot putt is essentially automatic (1.04 expected strokes), but it climbs steeply through the 5-15 foot range — the make-or-miss zone where great putters separate from the field. Beyond 30 feet, the curve flattens out: you'll almost always 2-putt.

Notice the small jumps: a 150-yard shot from rough costs 0.20 strokes more than the same shot from fairway. A 10-foot putt is only worth 0.50 strokes less than a 3-footer. These small differences add up across 70+ shots in a round.

The fine print

Precision varies by situation. Common shots like 150-yard approaches have hundreds of thousands of data points behind them. Rare shots — 500-yard drives, recovery from pine straw — rely on smoothing across nearby distances. The expected strokes value for a 501-yard shot is interpolated, not measured directly.

Context is averaged out. Expected strokes accounts for distance and lie, but not wind, slope, or pin position. Two 165-yard shots from the fairway are treated as equal, even if one is downwind to a back pin and the other is into wind to a front pin. These effects mostly average out across many shots, but they're a known blind spot — particularly at links courses.

Why this matters

Adding it all up

Strokes gained is the framework underneath modern golf analytics. Every player ranking, course comparison, and statistical analysis you see on tour broadcasts — and every chart we publish at The Caddie Desk — rests on this foundation.

It's not perfect. Wind isn't accounted for. Slope isn't accounted for. Some shots are rare enough that their expected strokes value is more interpolation than measurement. But across thousands of shots, the system reveals the truth about where each player's skill lives.
