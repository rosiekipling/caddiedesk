What makes a golf course?

419 words · June 2026 · Rosie Kipling

Watch enough PGA Tour golf and you start to feel which courses reward which skills. Augusta is where short irons matter. The Open is where wind makes everyone vulnerable. Plantation Course at Kapalua is where birdies pile up faster than at any other event on tour. But that's all feel. What does the data say?

We trained a model on 287,000 player-rounds from 2012-2025 to learn what predicts good play. Then we measured how much each skill matters at each course. Here's what we found.

The shorter game

If course character emerges anywhere, it's around the green. The spread here is wider — courses are more distinguishable on the approach/putting axis than on the driving axis.

Le Golf National

The venue for the 2018 Ryder Cup is the most demanding test of approach play AND putting on tour. The narrowest target, the trickiest greens.

Augusta National

Augusta sits in the bottom-left — surprising for a course famous for its putting. But the field is so tight at the Masters that no single stat clearly separates winners from losers. The differentiation happens elsewhere.

Does longer mean bombers win?

>

Logic vs data

Logic says: longer course = bombers' paradise. The data says: not really.

The flat line

Among well-sampled tour venues, course yardage explains almost nothing about how much driving distance matters (r = 0.04). The line is nearly flat.

Augusta is long but not for bombers

The longest courses on tour aren't where bombers thrive — Augusta National is 7,545 yards and rewards distance less than most short courses.

The conclusion

What predicts whether bombers win at a course isn't its length. It's everything else.
