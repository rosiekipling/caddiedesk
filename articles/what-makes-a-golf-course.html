What makes a golf course?

707 words · June 2026 · Rosie Kipling

Watch enough PGA Tour golf and you start to feel which courses reward which skills. Augusta is where the iron play and short game decide it. The Open is won on and around the green. Plantation Course at Kapalua is where birdies pile up faster than at any other event on tour. But that's all feel. What does the data say?

We trained a model on 287,000 player-rounds from 2012-2025 to learn what predicts good play. Then we measured how much each skill matters at each course. Here's what we found.

Course Types: What's the difference?

Course Type

Parkland

67 courses

Parkland

The default. Parkland defines what 'average' looks like — close to the baseline on every stat. Most PGA Tour stops are some variation of parkland.

Stadium

Pete Dye's TPC courses reward precision over power. Approach play is the standout skill — demanding pins and severe surrounds put a premium on hitting the right part of the green. Distance matters least; the fairways look wide, but water and waste bunkers keep accuracy honest.

Coastal

Coastal courses reward the short game and approach play more than power. Around-the-green skill and approach both rank well above average, while driving accuracy sits below it — these aren't courses where finding the fairway is the whole battle. The standout skill is scrambling: when wind pushes the ball off small targets, the players who chip and putt their way back into the hole are the ones who win.

Links

Links golf is decided on and around the green. Around-the-green skill and putting both rank in the top quarter of all courses — the firm, undulating surfaces put a premium on touch. Distance helps too (firm fairways add runout, wide corridors invite aggression), but approach play matters less: links approaches often aren't approach shots at all. Players bump it on from 20 yards short, a skill closer to chipping than iron play.

Parkland barely moved the needle — it sat on the average line across every skill. That's because "parkland" lumps together a century of design philosophy. Split it by era, and a clear pattern emerges.

Inside parkland: three eras of design

Parkland Era

Golden Age

The Golden Age

Pre-1940 designs by Ross, MacKenzie, Tillinghast. Driving accuracy ranks highest here — these courses pre-date the modern bomber and reward finding the right position over raw power. Approach importance is the lowest of the three eras.

The Modern Era

1940-1990. Robert Trent Jones Sr, Pete Dye, Jack Nicklaus. The most balanced profile of the three eras — every skill sits close to the tour average. Approach play edges ahead as courses get longer and more demanding.

The Contemporary Era

Post-1990. Coore & Crenshaw, Tom Doak, Kyle Phillips. Distance ranks highest while the scoring skills — approach, around-green, putting — all rank below average. These long, modern layouts reward power off the tee more than touch around the greens.

The outliers

The outliers: What makes a legend?

Some courses refuse to fit a category — and they're the most interesting tests in golf. Three of them break the mould entirely.

Outlier

Augusta National

Augusta National

Augusta rewards two skills above all others: approach play and around-the-green touch, both ranking among the highest of any course. The demanding pins reward precise irons, and the slopes and false fronts demand creative recovery when you miss. Distance and accuracy off the tee matter surprisingly little — at the Masters, the tournament is won with the short clubs.

Plantation Course

Kapalua tests one thing above all: power. Driving distance ranks in the top third, but every other skill sits below average — and short game ranks near the bottom. With winners averaging 23-under, the differentiator isn't grinding out pars or scrambling. The best players don't miss the notoriously large greens; they just out-hit each other.

The Old Course

The Old Course is a paradox: hit it long, miss it however you want, then hole the putts. Driving distance and putting both rank near the very top, but driving accuracy and approach play rank near the bottom. Wide fairways and no rough remove the penalty for missing; massive, undulating greens — some over 80 yards across — make lag putting the decisive skill. Approach barely registers because links approaches aren't typical approach shots.
